HOW DID DIAL-UP WORK?

🖥 Your computer...

Your computer creates digital/binary data using 1s and 0s. To access the internet during the dial-up era, that data first had to leave your computer and travel through older telephone infrastructure.

They use the same frequency bands as voice calls, hence the interference when picking up the phone.

The problem was that telephone lines were designed for voice, not for direct computer data, so the signal had to be converted first.

📟 The modem...

The modem acted as a translator. It converted your computer’s digital signals into analogue sounds that could travel through a phone line (the copper wires). This process is called modulation.

It also converted incoming analogue signals back into digital data your computer could understand. This process is called demodulation.

Fun Fact: The term "modem" comes from "modulator-demodulator."

☎ The phone line...

The connection travelled through the same telephone line people used for calls. That is why using the internet could block the phone and why calls could interrupt the connection.

🏢 The ISP server...

The Internet Service Provider received your connection, checked your user account, and connected you onward to the wider internet.

🌍 Internet...

Once connected, your data could travel across networks to reach websites, email servers, and online services, although much more slowly than modern broadband.

Why Dial-Up Was Problematic

Very slow speed

  • Webpages took a long time to load
  • Downloading files could take hours or days
  • Streaming media was nearly impossible

Tied-up phones lines

  • Could not use phone line while online!
  • Had to wait for the line to be available
  • Interrupted calls were common

Long connection times

  • Connection process could take several minutes
  • Users had to wait for the modem to establish a connection
  • Time-consuming process with frequent disconnections

Unstable connection

  • Connection would frequently drop and depended on distance to ISP
  • Users had to re-establish the connection often
  • Difficult to maintain consistent connectivity

Expensive usage

  • High pay-per-minute or data usage fees
  • Additional costs for phone line usage
  • Expensive setup and maintenance !

Security limitations

  • Limited encryption and security measures
  • Prone to hacking and unauthorized access
  • Difficult to protect sensitive information

THE EVOLUTION OF THE INTERNET

The Dial-Up Era (1990s - 2000s)

Key Features:

  • Used existing telephone lines and infrastructure (only way to easily connect the general public)
  • Used famous 'handshake' connection sound (skrrr boop boop)
  • AOL (America Online) was the most popular internet service provider
  • Pay-per-minute pricing or limited monthly hours
  • Could not use phone line while online! (because they used the same frequency and line)

The DSL & Cable Era (2000s - 2010s)

Key Features:

  • Used phone lines but at high frequency (could now use phone lines!)
  • Always on! No dialing required!
  • Speed depended on the distance from the telephone exchange
  • Required a special filter to separate voice and data signals

The Broadband Era (2010s - Present)

Key Features:

  • Cable internet shared TV infrastructure
  • Made video streaming possible!
  • No more cables thansk to WiFi!
  • Enabled social media and cloud services

The Fibre & 5G Era (2019 - present)

Key Features:

  • Data is transmitted as light through glass fibre cables, offering much higher speeds and lower latency than traditional copper wires
  • Nearly unlimited bandwidth
  • High-speed connectivity for both residential and commercial use
  • Enables 4K video streaming and other high-bandwidth applications eg. virtual reality and gaming
